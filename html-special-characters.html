Article 1: Special Characters by Webmonkey Staff

web monkey article

This one is so short, it barely qualifies as an article or blog post. But it caught my eye since it’s affiliated with Wired, which I try to read monthly. It looks to be mainly a list of characters and their corresponding HTML codes. It’s the first place I’ve heard mention of the ISO Latin-1 table, which will probably come in handy at some point. It doesn’t really offer a whole lot more info, but I bookmarked it for future reference. This webmonkey site actually looks to be a pretty badass resource. Lots of tutorials and cheat sheets and stuff. I’m glad I stumbled on it, because I bet I’ll get more use out of it as we get further into the class.


Article 2: HTML Special Characters by Neal Chester

ikreator article

This looks to be more of a self promo blog than an article. But it looks like a helpful resource of actual special characters and their html equivalents, as well as a few paragraphs about how to use them. This guy styled and curated a bunch of his most commonly used special characters and put them in one place, available to others to use. Very cool. I like how the web dev community does stuff like this, similar to the graphic design community and free vector art, fonts, plugins, etc. It seems a bit backwards to start the blog post with all the codes, and then include his write ups at the bottom of the page. But, hey, who am I to complain when he’s taking the time to make this stuff available for everyone. It’s cool that he grouped them into HTML, CSS and JS, also. He gives you the UTF-8 code to drop in (which I remember hearing about in our first class.) And he explains how you should plug it in for both HTML and CSS. He also mentions that sometimes they don’t render properly across all browsers, so you need to cross check it yourself to be sure. This stuff will probably make more sense to me in a few months, once I’ve gotten my hands dirty a bit. But I understand it in principle right now.
